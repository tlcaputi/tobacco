Why this dataset?

Health economics research on tobacco policy requires knowing exactly which local jurisdictions have which types of tobacco control ordinances, and when they took effect. The primary existing source, the ANR Foundation U.S. Tobacco Control Laws Database, costs $5,000–$8,000+ per data report. This project builds a comparable open dataset covering all US jurisdictions.

This work was motivated by Abouk et al. (2023), which estimates the causal effects of e-cigarette taxes on youth tobacco use and controls for local tobacco control policies. Constructing population-weighted policy exposure measures requires jurisdiction-level coverage data — exactly what this dataset provides.

Methodology: Three-Pass Blind Search

Each jurisdiction is independently searched multiple times by separate AI agents with no knowledge of prior results. A majority-vote reconciliation determines which laws are included in the final dataset.

1

Enumerate

All counties and incorporated places from the Census Bureau (2020 PL 94-171), with FIPS codes and population.

2

Pass 1 & 2

Two independent blind searches of municipal codes, government websites, news, and policy databases. No knowledge of each other's results.

3

Reconcile

Disagreements get a tiebreaker (Pass 3). Laws found by 2+ of 3 passes are included. Single-pass findings are excluded.

Confidence levels
  • High Found by both Pass 1 and Pass 2 independently
  • Confirmed Found by 2 of 3 passes (tiebreaker resolved)
  • Low Found by only 1 pass — not included in final dataset

Data sources searched

Agents search across multiple sources for each jurisdiction:

  • Municipal code libraries: Municode, eCode360, American Legal Publishing, Qcode
  • Government websites: City/county official sites, meeting minutes, ordinance databases
  • Policy databases: ANR Foundation lists, Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids, Counter Tobacco
  • News and media: Local news coverage of ordinance adoption
  • State health departments: Tobacco prevention program records

Known limitations

  • State vs. local confusion: Agents sometimes attribute statewide laws to individual cities (the #1 error type). The three-pass methodology mitigates this but doesn't eliminate it.
  • County vs. city confusion: County ordinances may be incorrectly attributed to cities within that county.
  • Coverage: Only states with completed three-pass searches have reliable data. See the Pipeline page for current progress.
  • Temporal coverage: The dataset captures current/recent ordinances. Historical data (effective dates) is captured where available but coverage varies.
